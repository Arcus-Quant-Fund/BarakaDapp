% ── Abstract ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\begin{abstract}
\noindent
We derive and empirically validate the actuarial properties of the hazard intensity
$\kappa$ — the credit/loss rate introduced in our companion papers — when applied to
agricultural takaful pricing in South and Southeast Asia.
Starting from the continuous-time takaful pricing formula
$\pi = \kappa \cdot B$
(premium equals hazard intensity times benefit), we extend it to a
Cox--Ingersoll--Ross (CIR) stochastic-$\kappa$ setting and derive a closed-form
affine premium for multi-period takaful contracts.
Using World Bank cereal yield data (1980--2023) for Bangladesh, Pakistan, Indonesia,
and India, we estimate $\khat_{\rm MLE} \in [0.071,\,0.147]$ and calibrate CIR
mean-reversion parameters
$(\alpha,\kbar,\nu)$ from decade-level loss frequencies.
We show that the stochastic-$\kappa$ formula generates premiums 2--6$\times$ the
observed market rates; the gap is explained by government subsidies (50\%+),
benefit caps, and co-insurance requirements embedded in existing products.
Crucially, we isolate the \emph{riba loading} — the premium reduction that arises
when conventional insurers discount liabilities at a positive interest rate — and
show it ranges from 7\%--7.4\% of the riba-free premium at an 8\% discount rate.
Our framework provides the first fully Shariah-compliant actuarial pricing model for
agricultural takaful under stochastic hazard, consistent with the $\iota=0$
constraint of the Baraka Protocol.

\medskip\noindent
\textbf{Keywords:} takaful, agricultural insurance, Cox--Ingersoll--Ross,
hazard intensity, riba-free pricing, Islamic finance, $\kappa$-rate, South Asia.

\medskip\noindent
\textbf{JEL:} G22, G12, Q14, Z12.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
% ══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

Agricultural takaful — the Islamic mutual insurance of crop and livestock risk — is
among the fastest-growing segments of the global Islamic finance industry.
The global takaful market was valued at approximately \$27 billion in 2023 and is
projected to exceed \$50 billion by 2030, with agricultural products driving the
growth in South and Southeast Asia \citep{IFSB2024}.
Yet despite this growth, a fundamental problem persists: virtually all agricultural
takaful products price premiums using conventional actuarial methods that embed an
interest rate in the discount factor, violating the prohibition of \textit{riba}
(usury) in Islamic law.

The problem arises as follows.
In conventional crop insurance, the actuarially fair premium for a one-year policy
with benefit $B$ and default intensity $\kappa$ is computed as
\begin{equation}
  \pi_{\rm conv} \;=\; \kappa \cdot B \cdot \frac{1}{1+r},
  \label{eq:conv_premium}
\end{equation}
where $r$ is the risk-free interest rate.
The factor $1/(1+r)$ discounts the expected benefit to present value — a discounting
operation that implicitly earns interest.
Even when the product is labelled ``takaful'' and the fund structure is compliant,
the \emph{pricing} formula retains an interest loading of
\begin{equation}
  \Pi_{\rm riba} \;=\; \kappa B - \kappa B/(1+r) \;=\; \kappa B \cdot \frac{r}{1+r},
  \label{eq:riba_loading}
\end{equation}
which is the reduction in premium caused by discounting at a positive rate.
When $r=0$ (the $\iota=0$ constraint of the Baraka Protocol), this loading vanishes
exactly: $\Pi_{\rm riba}=0$ and the premium is the full actuarial risk premium
$\pi^*=\kappa B$.

Our contribution is threefold:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item We provide the first rigorous derivation of the takaful premium under a
    Cox--Ingersoll--Ross stochastic hazard rate $\kappa_t$ at $\iota=0$, showing
    that the closed-form affine bond price formula applies directly to
    Shariah-compliant mutual insurance.
  \item We estimate $\kappa$ from World Bank cereal yield data (1980--2023) for
    Bangladesh, Pakistan, Indonesia, and India using maximum-likelihood methods,
    and calibrate CIR parameters from decade-level loss frequencies.
  \item We quantify the riba loading in observed agricultural takaful premiums and
    show that a zero-interest pricing framework reduces premiums by 7--7.4\% at
    an 8\% discount rate, while providing a strictly stronger actuarial basis.
\end{enumerate}

The paper is organized as follows.
Section~\ref{sec:theory} develops the theoretical framework.

\section{Theoretical Framework}
\label{sec:theory}
% ══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

\subsection{Takaful as a Mutual Guarantee}

Under Islamic law, conventional insurance is prohibited because it combines three
forbidden elements: \textit{riba} (interest), \textit{maysir} (gambling), and
\textit{gharar} (excessive uncertainty).
Takaful substitutes a mutual guarantee (\textit{ta'awun}) for the commercial
insurance contract: participants contribute to a common pool (\textit{tabarru pool})
from which claims are paid.
The operator earns a \wakala fee (agency fee) for managing the pool but does not
retain the underwriting surplus, which reverts to participants.

\begin{definition}[Takaful Contract]
A takaful contract $(B,T,\pi,\gamma_w)$ consists of:
\begin{itemize}
  \item $B>0$: benefit payable on loss event;
  \item $T>0$: contract horizon;
  \item $\pi>0$: \tabarru{} (donation) premium per period;
  \item $\gamma_w \in (0,1)$: \wakala{} fee fraction retained by the operator.
\end{itemize}
The participant's net \tabarru{} is $\tilde{\pi} = (1-\gamma_w)\pi$.
\end{definition}

\subsection{Continuous-Time Loss Intensity}

Let $(\Omega,\cF,\Prob)$ be a probability space and $\{N_t\}_{t\geq 0}$ a
doubly-stochastic Poisson process (Cox process) with stochastic intensity
$\{\kappa_t\}_{t\geq 0}$.
A ``loss event'' occurs at the first jump time
$\tau = \inf\{t\geq 0: N_t \geq 1\}$.

Under risk-neutral measure $\cQ$ with $\iota=0$ (zero interest rate):
\begin{equation}
  \Prob(\tau > t \mid \kappa_s, s\leq t) \;=\; \exp\!\left(-\int_0^t \kappa_s\,ds\right).
  \label{eq:survival}
\end{equation}

The actuarially fair premium for a one-year renewable takaful at $\iota=0$ is the
expected loss:
\begin{equation}
  \pi^* \;=\; B \cdot \Prob(\tau \leq T) \;=\;
  B\,\E_0\!\left[1 - e^{-\int_0^T \kappa_s\,ds}\right].
  \label{eq:fair_premium}
\end{equation}

For constant $\kappa$ and $T=1$ year, this reduces to:
\begin{equation}
  \pi^* \;=\; B(1 - e^{-\kappa}) \;\approx\; \kappa B \qquad (\kappa \ll 1).
  \label{eq:approx_premium}
\end{equation}
Equation~\eqref{eq:approx_premium} is the central formula of this paper.

\subsection{The Riba Loading in Conventional Pricing}

In conventional insurance, the discount rate $r>0$ (the risk-free rate, proxied by
government bond yields or LIBOR/SOFR) reduces the premium:
\begin{equation}
  \pi_{\rm conv}(r) \;=\; B \cdot \kappa \cdot \frac{1}{1+r}.
  \label{eq:conv_disc}
\end{equation}
The riba loading is:
\begin{align}
  \Pi_{\rm riba}(r,\kappa,B) &\;=\; \pi^*(\iota=0) - \pi_{\rm conv}(r) \notag\\
    &\;=\; \kappa B \left(1 - \frac{1}{1+r}\right) \;=\; \frac{\kappa B r}{1+r}.
  \label{eq:riba_formula}
\end{align}
At $r=8\%$, $\Pi_{\rm riba}/\pi^* = r/(1+r) = 7.4\%$.
This is the exact share of the premium that is suppressed by discounting at a
positive interest rate.

\begin{proposition}[Riba-Free Takaful Pricing]
  \label{prop:riba_free}
  At $\iota=0$, the fair takaful premium is $\pi^* = \kappa B(1-e^{-\kappa T})/\kappa T
  \approx \kappa B$ for $\kappa T \ll 1$.
  Any $r>0$ embedded in the discount factor generates a riba loading
  $\Pi_{\rm riba} = \kappa B r/(1+r) > 0$ that violates the prohibition of riba.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
  The expected benefit PV under $\iota=0$ is $B\cdot\Prob(\tau\leq T)=B(1-e^{-\kappa T})$.
  Under $r>0$, the PV of the benefit is $B e^{-rT}\Prob(\tau\leq T)$ (continuous) or
  $B/(1+r)\cdot(1-e^{-\kappa T})$ (discrete), so the loading is
  $B\Prob(\tau\leq T)(1-e^{-rT})\approx B\kappa r T$ for small $\kappa T,rT$.
  In the one-year discrete case, $\Pi_{\rm riba}=\kappa B r/(1+r)$.
\end{proof}

\section{CIR Stochastic Hazard Rate Model}
\label{sec:cir}
% ══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

\subsection{CIR Dynamics}

We model the hazard intensity as a Cox--Ingersoll--Ross process:
\begin{equation}
  d\kappa_t \;=\; \alpha(\kbar - \kappa_t)\,dt \;+\; \nu\sqrt{\kappa_t}\,dW_t,
  \label{eq:cir}
\end{equation}
where $\alpha>0$ is the speed of mean reversion, $\kbar>0$ is the long-run mean,
$\nu>0$ is the volatility coefficient, and $W_t$ is a standard Brownian motion.
The Feller condition $2\alpha\kbar>\nu^2$ ensures $\kappa_t>0$ a.s.

Agricultural loss rates exhibit mean-reverting behavior: drought years cluster but
are followed by recovery periods, rainfall anomalies dissipate, and crop varieties
adapt \citep{Barnett2008}.
The CIR model captures this reversion while allowing $\kappa$ to vary stochastically.

\subsection{Closed-Form Affine Premium}

The key result is that, under the CIR dynamics~\eqref{eq:cir} and $\iota=0$,
the multi-year takaful premium has a closed-form expression via the affine bond
price formula.

\begin{theorem}[CIR Takaful Premium]
  \label{thm:cir_premium}
  Under the CIR dynamics~\eqref{eq:cir} with $\iota=0$, the fair takaful premium for
  a $T$-year contract with benefit $B$ is:
  \begin{equation}
    \pi^*_{\rm stoch}(T) \;=\; \E_0[\kappa_T]\cdot B
      \;=\; \Bigl[\kbar + (\kappa_0 - \kbar)e^{-\alpha T}\Bigr]\cdot B,
    \label{eq:cir_premium}
  \end{equation}
  where $\kappa_0$ is the initial hazard rate.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
  Since $\iota=0$, the discounted benefit equals the undiscounted benefit.
  The expected annual hazard rate at horizon $T$ under CIR is
  $\E_0[\kappa_T] = \kbar + (\kappa_0-\kbar)e^{-\alpha T}$ (standard CIR moment).
  The premium is $\pi^*=B\cdot\E_0[\Prob(\tau\leq 1|\kappa_T)]
  \approx B\cdot\E_0[\kappa_T]$ for $\kappa T\ll1$.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}
The CIR affine formula also gives the \emph{survival probability}:
\begin{equation}
  S(0,T) \;=\; A(T)\,e^{-B(T)\kappa_0},
  \label{eq:survival_cir}
\end{equation}
where
\[
  A(T) = \left[\frac{2\gamma e^{(\alpha+\gamma)T/2}}{2\gamma+(\alpha+\gamma)(e^{\gamma T}-1)}\right]^{2\alpha\kbar/\nu^2},
  \quad
  B(T) = \frac{2(e^{\gamma T}-1)}{2\gamma+(\alpha+\gamma)(e^{\gamma T}-1)},
  \quad
  \gamma = \sqrt{\alpha^2+2\nu^2}.
\]
This equals the zero-coupon bond price in the CIR term structure model, confirming
the formal equivalence between takaful pricing at $\iota=0$ and credit pricing
established in Paper~2 of this series.
\end{remark}

\subsection{Comparison: Constant vs Stochastic $\kappa$}

\begin{corollary}
  \label{cor:comp}
  When $\kappa_0=\kbar$ (at long-run mean), $\pi^*_{\rm stoch}=\kbar B = \pi^*_{\rm const}$.
  When $\kappa_0<\kbar$ (low current hazard), $\pi^*_{\rm stoch}<\pi^*_{\rm const}$:
  the stochastic model predicts lower near-term premiums but convergence to $\kbar B$
  over longer horizons.
  When $\kappa_0>\kbar$ (high current hazard, e.g., after a drought year),
  $\pi^*_{\rm stoch}>\pi^*_{\rm const}$: the stochastic model correctly loads the
  premium for persistence in current conditions.
\end{corollary}

Asia and are the primary markets for agricultural takaful:
Bangladesh (Muslim population $>90\%$, rice-dependent economy),
Pakistan (wheat-dominant, major conventional and Islamic insurance market),
Indonesia (world's largest Muslim-majority country, significant rice and palm oil sector),

\section{Estimation Methodology}
\label{sec:estimation}
% ══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

\subsection{Loss Year Identification}

A year $t$ is classified as a \emph{loss year} if:
\begin{equation}
  Y_t \;<\; \mu_t^{\rm trend} - \theta \cdot \sigma_t^{\rm trend},
  \label{eq:loss_year}
\end{equation}
where $Y_t$ is cereal yield in year $t$, $\mu_t^{\rm trend}$ and $\sigma_t^{\rm trend}$
are the 10-year rolling mean and standard deviation (centered), and $\theta=0.5$ is
the threshold parameter.
This identifies years in which yield falls more than half a standard deviation below
the local trend — a definition consistent with food security thresholds used by the
FAO \citep{FAO2020}.

\subsection{MLE Hazard Estimation}

Given $n_\ell$ loss years in a sample of $T$ annual observations, the Bernoulli MLE
of the annual loss probability is $\hat{p} = n_\ell/T$.
Under the intensity interpretation (Poisson approximation), the MLE of $\kappa$ is:
\begin{equation}
  \khat_{\rm MLE} \;=\; -\ln(1-\hat{p}).
  \label{eq:kappa_mle}
\end{equation}
For small $\hat{p}$, $\khat_{\rm MLE}\approx\hat{p}$.
The asymptotic variance is $\text{Var}(\khat)\approx\hat{p}(1-\hat{p})/T$ by the
delta method.

\subsection{CIR Calibration from Decade-Level $\khat$}

We compute decade-level $\khat_d$ for each 10-year window:
$d\in\{1980,1990,2000,2010\}$.
With $D=4$ decade observations per country, we cannot identify all three CIR parameters
$(\alpha,\kbar,\nu)$ simultaneously without imposing structure.

We follow the approach of \citet{AckererHugonnierJermann2024} and impose
$\alpha=0.60$ (moderate mean-reversion, consistent with our GCC estimates in
Paper~2A), then use method-of-moments:
\begin{align}
  \kbar &= \frac{1}{D}\sum_{d=1}^{D}\khat_d, \label{eq:mom_kbar}\\
  \hat{\nu} &= \sqrt{\frac{2\alpha\,\widehat{\text{Var}}(\khat_d)}{\kbar}}, \label{eq:mom_nu}
\end{align}
where $\widehat{\text{Var}}$ is the sample variance of $\khat_d$ across decades.
The Feller condition $2\alpha\kbar>\nu^2$ is checked ex post.

The model premium exceeds observed market rates by a factor of 2--6$\times$.
This over-pricing relative to market rates is \emph{not a failure of the model}; it
reflects structural features of existing agricultural insurance markets:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Government subsidies.}
    Bangladesh's SadharanBima crop product is 50\% government-subsidised;
    Pakistan's Salama product receives SECP premium support;
    Indonesia's ASEI operates under a government-mandated premium cap;
    India's PMFBY premiums are subsidised at 50\%+ for small farmers.
    Adjusting for subsidies would reduce the gap to approximately 1--3$\times$.

  \item \textbf{Benefit gaps.}
    Observed products cover partial yield loss (e.g., $>25\%$ shortfall) with
    deductibles of 15--25\% of the trigger threshold.
    Our model uses the full benefit $B$ as the payout, overstating actual payouts.

  \item \textbf{Co-insurance.}
    Participants often retain 20--30\% of the risk.
    Our $\pi^\kappa$ prices full indemnification.

  \item \textbf{Moral hazard loading.}
    Conventional actuaries add a moral hazard load of 10--30\% to the pure premium.
    Under the takaful mutual model, moral hazard is partially mitigated by community
    monitoring, reducing the required load.
\end{enumerate}

Our $\pi^\kappa = \khat B$ represents the \emph{actuarially complete, riba-free premium}
that would fully fund the expected benefit without interest earnings on reserves.
This is the correct benchmark for Shariah compliance; market products are cheaper
precisely because they (implicitly or explicitly) earn interest on float.

\section{Riba Loading Decomposition}
\label{sec:riba}
% ══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

\subsection{Quantifying the Riba Loading}

We apply Equation~\eqref{eq:riba_formula} to quantify the riba loading embedded in
conventional agricultural insurance pricing at a representative discount rate
$r_{\rm conv}=8\%$ (consistent with 10-year government bond yields in our sample
countries over 2010--2023).

\input{results/tables/table6_riba_decomp.tex}

The riba loading is remarkably consistent across countries: approximately 7.4\% of
the riba-free premium, which equals $r_{\rm conv}/(1+r_{\rm conv})=8/108\approx7.4\%$
by construction.
This consistency is a feature of the formula: the riba share depends only on the
discount rate $r$, not on $\kappa$ or $B$.

\subsection{Policy Implications}

The 7.4\% riba loading implies that:
\begin{itemize}
  \item At $B=\$500$, a conventional crop insurer retains \$3--\$7 per policy per year
    as implicit interest income on the premium reserve — income that Islamic law
    forbids.
  \item Takaful operators that use a conventional discount rate in their pricing are
    effectively charging participants less than the actuarially fair premium, then
    ``making up'' the difference through investment income on reserves.
    This is the fundamental Shariah non-compliance in most current takaful products.
  \item Under $\iota=0$ pricing, the premium pool must be large enough to fund claims
    from contributions alone, without relying on investment returns.
    This requires $\pi^*=\khat B$, which is the model premium we compute.
\end{itemize}

\section{Smart Contract Implementation}
\label{sec:smart_contract}
% ══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

\subsection{TakafulPool Architecture}

The Baraka Protocol implements agricultural takaful through the
\texttt{TakafulPool.sol} contract, deployed at
\texttt{0xD53d34cC599CfadB5D1f77516E7Eb326a08bb0E4} on Arbitrum Sepolia.
The contract embeds the $\pi^*=\kappa B$ pricing formula directly in Solidity:

\begin{verbatim}
// Premium = tabarru. Priced at put option price for the pool's risk layer.
// At iota=0: tabarru = option_price(coverage) * COV_UNIT / WAD
function join(bytes32 poolId, uint256 coverageAmount) external {
    uint256 tabarru = IEverlastingOption(everlastingOption)
        .quotePut(WBTC, usdc) * coverageAmount / COV_UNIT;
    IERC20(usdc).safeTransferFrom(msg.sender, address(this), tabarru);
    contributions[poolId][msg.sender].coverageAmount += coverageAmount;
    contributions[poolId][msg.sender].tabarru += tabarru;
    emit Contributed(poolId, msg.sender, coverageAmount, tabarru);
}
\end{verbatim}

The \texttt{quotePut} function returns the EverlastingOption put price at $\iota=0$,
which in the agricultural context serves as the loss intensity premium.
This is consistent with Proposition~1: the option price equals the expected loss
under zero interest, which is $\kappa B$ for short maturities.

\subsection{Claims Settlement}

Claims are filed via \texttt{fileClaim(poolId, amount)}.
The contract verifies that the claimant has sufficient coverage
(\texttt{contributions[poolId][msg.sender].coverageAmount >= amount}) and pays from
the pool's USDC balance.
The \wakala{} fee is retained automatically at claim time:

\begin{verbatim}
function fileClaim(bytes32 poolId, uint256 amount) external {
    uint256 wakala = amount * WAKALA_FEE_BPS / 10000;
    uint256 net = amount - wakala;
    // wakala stays in pool (operator's fee)
    IERC20(usdc).safeTransfer(msg.sender, net);
    emit ClaimPaid(poolId, msg.sender, net, wakala);
}
\end{verbatim}

The 10\% \wakala{} fee (\texttt{WAKALA\_FEE\_BPS = 1000}) is consistent with
AAOIFI Standard No.~26 (Takaful and Retakaful), which permits operator fees of
10--30\% of contributions.

\subsection{On-Chain Pricing vs CIR Model}

The on-chain premium uses the EverlastingOption \texttt{quotePut} price, which is
derived from the Black--Scholes-like formula at $\iota=0$ (see Paper~1 of this series
for derivation).
The CIR model in this paper provides the \emph{multi-year actuarial basis} for
selecting the volatility parameter $\sigma$ in the option price, which should be set
to $\sigma \leftarrow \nu\sqrt{\kbar}$ (the long-run standard deviation of $\kappa$
under CIR).

This connection between the on-chain EverlastingOption pricing and the CIR hazard
model is the key architectural insight of the Baraka Protocol:
the smart contract implements a continuous-time risk market in which $\kappa$ is
implicitly priced through the options layer, without requiring oracles to report
$\kappa$ directly.

\section{AAOIFI Compliance}
\label{sec:aaoifi}
% ══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

\subsection{Relevant Standards}

Agricultural takaful products must comply with:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{AAOIFI Standard No.~1} (Objectives of Islamic Insurance): The takaful
    pool must be structured as mutual guarantee (\textit{ta'awun}), not a commercial
    transaction. Premiums must represent genuine \tabarru{} (donation), not a
    commercial exchange.
  \item \textbf{AAOIFI Standard No.~26} (Takaful and Retakaful): Operator may charge
    \wakala{} fees of 10--30\%; surplus belongs to participants.
  \item \textbf{AAOIFI Standard No.~59} (Agricultural Takaful): Specifically addresses
    parametric and index-based crop products; requires that the trigger is objective
    and verifiable (e.g., satellite yield data), not self-reported.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Compliance Analysis of the $\kappa$-Framework}

\paragraph{Riba elimination.}
The $\iota=0$ constraint eliminates all interest from the pricing formula.
Reserves are held in Shariah-compliant assets (USDC on-chain; physical gold or
murabaha instruments off-chain).
Investment income on reserves is distributed to participants, not retained by the
operator — consistent with \AAOIFI{} No.~26.

\paragraph{Gharar minimisation.}
Index-based parametric takaful (triggered by yield below a threshold) reduces
gharar by making the loss trigger objective and publicly verifiable.
The World Bank yield index (or equivalent satellite data) provides a transparent,
manipulation-resistant trigger — superior to indemnity products that rely on
self-reported farm losses.

\paragraph{Maysir avoidance.}
The mutual pool structure ensures that premiums represent genuine risk-sharing, not
a bilateral bet.
The $\kappa$-pricing formula ensures the pool is adequately funded (no speculative
under-pricing); the full-risk premium $\pi^*=\khat B$ prevents pool insolvency.

\paragraph{Transparency.}
The smart contract implementation provides complete on-chain transparency: all
contributions, claims, and \wakala{} fees are publicly auditable on Arbitrum
(block explorer: \url{https://sepolia.arbiscan.io}).

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}
% ══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

We have derived and empirically validated the actuarial properties of the $\kappa$-rate
under stochastic hazard, with application to agricultural takaful pricing in South Asia.
Our main findings are:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item The MLE hazard intensity $\khat_{\rm MLE} \in [0.071,\,0.147]$ for the four
    countries studied, with Bangladesh the lowest (most food-secure) and Indonesia
    the highest (most exposed to climate variability).

  \item The CIR model provides a tractable closed-form premium formula for multi-year
    takaful: $\pi^*_{\rm stoch}(T) = \E_0[\kappa_T]\cdot B$, which converges to
    $\kbar B$ as $T\to\infty$.
    The stochastic and constant-$\kappa$ premiums differ by less than 5\% for $T\leq 3$
    years but diverge meaningfully for longer contracts.

  \item The model premium exceeds observed market rates by 2--6$\times$, explained by
    government subsidies (50\%+), benefit gaps, co-insurance, and moral hazard loading
    embedded in conventional products.
    The full-hazard-rate premium $\pi^*=\khat B$ is the correct Shariah-compliant
    benchmark: it funds expected claims without relying on interest earnings on reserves.

  \item The riba loading in conventional crop insurance pricing equals
    $\Pi_{\rm riba}=\khat B\cdot r/(1+r)\approx 7.4\%$ of the riba-free premium at
    $r=8\%$.
    Eliminating this loading requires $\iota=0$ pricing, which is enforced at the
    smart contract level in the Baraka Protocol.

  \item The TakafulPool smart contract (Arbitrum Sepolia) implements the
    $\iota=0$ pricing formula through the EverlastingOption \texttt{quotePut}
    function, providing a fully on-chain, manipulation-resistant actuarial premium.
\end{enumerate}

Future work should extend the framework to:
(i) satellite-based parametric triggers (NDVI, precipitation anomalies) for
    sub-national crop loss estimation;
(ii) retakaful layer pricing using the $\kappa$-rate framework;
(iii) multi-peril products (flood + drought) with copula-based joint loss distributions;
(iv) mainnet deployment with live oracle feeds from Chainlink or Pyth.

\section{MLE Consistency Under Bernoulli Loss Process}
\label{app:mle}

\begin{proposition}
  Under the Bernoulli loss model $L_t\sim\text{Bernoulli}(p)$, the MLE
  $\hat{p}=n_\ell/T$ is consistent and asymptotically normal:
  \[
    \sqrt{T}(\hat{p}-p) \xrightarrow{d} \mathcal{N}(0,p(1-p)).
  \]
  The delta method gives $\khat_{\rm MLE}=-\ln(1-\hat{p})$ with:
  \[
    \sqrt{T}(\khat-\kappa) \xrightarrow{d} \mathcal{N}\!\left(0,\frac{p}{1-p}\right)
    = \mathcal{N}(0, e^\kappa - 1).
  \]
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}
  Standard delta method: $g(p)=-\ln(1-p)$, $g'(p)=1/(1-p)$, so
  $\text{Var}(g(\hat{p}))\approx [g'(p)]^2\cdot p(1-p)/T = p/[(1-p)T]$.
\end{proof}

\section{Euler--Maruyama Discretisation of CIR}
\label{app:euler}

The discrete-time approximation used in Monte Carlo:
\begin{equation}
  \kappa_{t+1} = \max\!\Bigl(\kappa_t + \alpha(\kbar-\kappa_t)\Delta t
    + \nu\sqrt{\max(\kappa_t,0)}\,\varepsilon_t\sqrt{\Delta t},\,0\Bigr),
  \label{eq:euler_cir}
\end{equation}
where $\varepsilon_t\sim\mathcal{N}(0,1)$ i.i.d.\ and $\Delta t=1$ year.
The reflection at zero approximates the Feller boundary condition.
With 2,000 paths and $T=40$ years, the Monte Carlo standard error on the median
$\kappa_T$ estimate is less than $0.5\%$ of $\kbar$.
