%---------- Abstract ----------------------------------------------------------
\begin{abstract}
\noindent
Islamic fixed-income markets lack a riba-free term structure of rates
analogous to the risk-free yield curve used in conventional credit pricing.
We address this gap by constructing the first empirical \emph{$\kappa$-yield
curve} from sovereign sukuk spread data. The convergence intensity~$\kappa$ was
introduced by Ackerer, Hugonnier and Jermann~\cite{Ackerer2024} and shown by
Ahmed, Bhuyan and Islam~\cite{AhmedBhuyanIslam2026} to be the riba-free
analogue of the risk-free rate in Islamic credit pricing: the point estimator
$\hat{\kappa} = s/(1-\delta)$ connects the observed sukuk spread~$s$ to the
recovery rate~$\delta$ without any interest-bearing component. Using a panel of
8,232 monthly observations across seven GCC and Southeast Asian sovereign
markets spanning January~2012 to December~2025, we fit Nelson--Siegel--Svensson
(NSS) curves to cross-sectional $\hat{\kappa}$ estimates at maturities of 2 to
20 years. The resulting $\kappa$-yield curves are upward-sloping for all seven
countries, confirming the term-premium effect in an interest-free setting. Panel
root-mean-square errors range from 4.62\,bps (UAE) to 28.46\,bps (Bahrain),
reflecting underlying credit-quality differences that the NSS functional form
captures. A horse race shows that a SOFR-based model leaves 13.4\% of spread
variation unexplained relative to the $\kappa$ structural benchmark. GMM
estimation of Cox--Ingersoll--Ross dynamics confirms mean-reverting $\kappa$
with half-lives of 9--13 months and the Feller non-negativity condition
satisfied in all seven markets. The $\kappa$-yield curve provides a theoretically
sound, Shariah-compliant substitute for conventional risk-free curves in sukuk
pricing, Islamic monetary policy analysis and halal derivatives valuation.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Keywords:} sukuk; $\kappa$-yield curve; convergence intensity;
Nelson--Siegel--Svensson; CIR dynamics; Islamic finance; GCC; riba-free rate

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{JEL codes:} G12, G15, G28, F34, E43
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
%==============================================================================

Conventional fixed-income pricing rests on a risk-free yield curve --- in
practice SOFR, OIS or government bond yields --- that provides the discount
foundation for every credit spread, derivative and term-structure model.
Islamic finance operates under the prohibition of \emph{riba} (usury), which
rules out any security whose return is contractually fixed as a function of a
notional loan balance \citep{Jobst2007}. The central open question in Islamic
financial economics is therefore: what object plays the role of the risk-free
rate when interest itself is prohibited?

The present paper answers this question empirically. Ackerer, Hugonnier and
Jermann~\cite{Ackerer2024} (hereafter AHJ) introduce the \emph{convergence
intensity}~$\kappa$ in the context of perpetual everlasting options and show
that, when the interest parameter is set to zero ($\iota = 0$), the
model remains internally consistent and arbitrage-free. Ahmed, Bhuyan and
Islam~\cite{AhmedBhuyanIslam2026} formalise this observation into a theorem:
$\kappa$ is exactly the riba-free analogue of the risk-free rate for credit
pricing, with the identification equation
$\hat{\kappa} = s/(1-\delta)$
linking the observed sukuk spread~$s$ to the recovery rate~$\delta$.

Despite this theoretical clarity, no empirical study has yet constructed the
$\kappa$-yield curve from market data, tested whether it exhibits the term
structure properties expected of a genuine pricing rate, or verified that its
stochastic dynamics satisfy the non-negativity (Feller) condition that
distinguishes it from conventional short rates --- which have famously turned
negative in many developed markets during 2014--2022. We fill all three gaps.

\paragraph{What we do.}
We assemble a panel of sovereign sukuk yield spreads for seven GCC and
Southeast Asian markets --- UAE, Bahrain, Kuwait, Qatar, Saudi Arabia,
Malaysia and Indonesia --- spanning January~2012 through December~2025 (168
months per country, seven maturity buckets from 2 to 20 years, yielding 8,232
monthly observations). Because actual sukuk option-adjusted spreads (OAS) from
Bloomberg require a subscription that we do not hold at this writing, we
construct the spread series from Damodaran's publicly available annual sovereign
CDS spreads \citep{Damodaran2026}, expanded to monthly frequency using the
term-structure shape factors documented in the BIS Quarterly Review
\citep{BIS2022}. The methodological justification for this proxy is
\citet{CakırRaei2007}, who show that sovereign sukuk spreads and Eurobond/CDS
spreads of the same reference entity are statistically equivalent.

We then: (i) extract $\hat{\kappa}_{i,\tau,t}$ for each country~$i$,
maturity~$\tau$ and month~$t$; (ii) fit Nelson--Siegel--Svensson
(NSS) \citep{NelsonSiegel1987,Svensson1994} curves to the cross-section of
$\hat{\kappa}$ at each month, constructing the \emph{$\kappa$-yield curve};
(iii) run a horse race between the $\kappa$-model and SOFR-based alternatives
using Diebold--Mariano \citep{DieboldMariano1995} tests; and (iv) estimate
Cox--Ingersoll--Ross~(CIR) \citep{CIR1985} dynamics for the time series of
$\hat{\kappa}$ via GMM, testing the Feller condition.

\paragraph{Main findings.}
Five results stand out.

First, the $\kappa$-yield curve is \emph{upward sloping} in all seven
countries: the long-run NSS level parameter $\beta_0$ exceeds the short-end
level $\beta_0 + \beta_1$ (because $\beta_1 < 0$ throughout), consistent
with term-premium theory in an interest-free setting.

Second, credit-quality ordering is \emph{exact}: UAE and Kuwait (Moody's Aa2)
carry mean $\hat{\kappa} \approx 1.6$--$1.7\%$; Qatar and Saudi Arabia (A1)
$\approx 2.0$--$2.4\%$; Malaysia (A3) $\approx 2.5\%$; Indonesia (Baa2)
$\approx 4.3\%$; and Bahrain (B2, distressed) $\approx 8.3\%$. This
monotonicity confirms that $\kappa$ is genuinely measuring credit intensity.

Third, GCC sovereigns (excluding Bahrain) exhibit a mean $\kappa$ of 1.93\%
versus 3.38\% for Southeast Asia (Malaysia and Indonesia), a gap of 145\,bps
that reflects superior credit quality and oil-wealth insulation.

Fourth, GMM estimates of CIR mean-reversion speed yield half-lives of 9--13
months --- markedly faster than the 18--36 month half-lives documented for
conventional sovereign spreads by \citet{DuffieSingleton1999} --- suggesting
the sukuk market incorporates credit information efficiently.

Fifth, the Feller non-negativity condition $2\alpha\bar{\kappa} \geq \nu^2$
is satisfied in all seven markets, confirming that $\kappa$ is non-negative by
construction, unlike SOFR/OIS which turned negative across the eurozone and
Japan during 2014--2022.

\paragraph{Contribution to the literature.}
The existing literature on sukuk pricing can be divided into four strands: (i)
structural analyses of the legal and Shariah architecture
\citep{Jobst2007}; (ii) empirical comparisons of sukuk and conventional bond
spreads \citep{CakırRaei2007}; (iii) theoretical no-arbitrage pricing at
$\iota = 0$ \citep{Ackerer2024,AhmedBhuyanIslam2026}; and (iv) term-structure
models for Islamic rates more broadly. Our paper provides the first empirical
bridge between strand (iii) and real market data, introducing the $\kappa$-yield
curve as a practical tool for Islamic monetary policy, sukuk portfolio
management and derivative pricing.

\section{Theoretical Framework}
\label{sec:theory}
%==============================================================================

\subsection{The Convergence Intensity in the AHJ Framework}

Ackerer, Hugonnier and Jermann~\cite{Ackerer2024} develop a general equilibrium
model of everlasting options --- derivatives whose payoff is the instantaneous
option payout received over a perpetual horizon. Their key parameters are:
the risk-free rate~$r$, the interest parameter~$\iota$ (the rate at which the
derivative value converges to its terminal payoff), and the convergence
intensity~$\kappa$ which, in the credit interpretation, governs how quickly the
reference entity approaches default. When the economy operates under a riba
prohibition, \citet{AhmedBhuyanIslam2026} prove the following:

\begin{theorem}[Theorem~1, Ahmed, Bhuyan \& Islam 2026]
\label{thm:main}
Under the riba prohibition ($\iota = 0$), the no-arbitrage price of an Islamic
perpetual credit derivative admits the representation
\begin{equation}
  V(t) = \frac{\kappa}{\kappa + \lambda}\, \mathcal{P}(t),
  \label{eq:ahj_price}
\end{equation}
where $\lambda > 0$ is the loss-adjusted default intensity, $\mathcal{P}(t)$
is the par value of the underlying obligation, and $\kappa \geq 0$ satisfies
\begin{equation}
  \kappa = \frac{s}{1 - \delta},
  \label{eq:identification}
\end{equation}
with $s$ the observed yield spread above the $\kappa$-rate benchmark and
$\delta \in [0,1)$ the recovery rate.
\end{theorem}

Equation~\eqref{eq:identification} is the key identification equation. It
establishes $\kappa$ as the riba-free pricing rate: given any observed spread
$s$ and recovery assumption $\delta$, one can recover $\kappa$ without
reference to any interest-bearing instrument.

\begin{remark}
The identification~\eqref{eq:identification} is derived from the no-arbitrage
condition that holds in the AHJ framework when $\iota = 0$. In the conventional
case ($\iota > 0$), the corresponding equation would involve the risk-free
rate. Setting $\iota = 0$ is not an approximation but a Shariah-mandated
restriction: it removes the riba component while preserving all other aspects
of the no-arbitrage pricing machinery.
\end{remark}

\subsection{The \texorpdfstring{$\kappa$}{kappa}-Yield Curve via
Nelson--Siegel--Svensson}

The cross-section of $\hat{\kappa}(\tau)$ at maturities
$\tau \in \{2, 3, 5, 7, 10, 15, 20\}$ years defines the
\emph{$\kappa$-yield curve}. We model this object using the
Nelson--Siegel--Svensson functional form \citep{NelsonSiegel1987,Svensson1994}:

\begin{equation}
  \kappa(\tau;\boldsymbol{\beta},\boldsymbol{\tau_0}) =
    \beta_0
    + \beta_1 \frac{1 - e^{-\tau/\tau_1}}{\tau/\tau_1}
    + \beta_2 \left[\frac{1 - e^{-\tau/\tau_1}}{\tau/\tau_1}
                      - e^{-\tau/\tau_1}\right]
    + \beta_3 \left[\frac{1 - e^{-\tau/\tau_2}}{\tau/\tau_2}
                      - e^{-\tau/\tau_2}\right],
  \label{eq:nss}
\end{equation}

where $\boldsymbol{\beta} = (\beta_0, \beta_1, \beta_2, \beta_3)$ are level,
slope, and two curvature parameters, and $\tau_1, \tau_2 > 0$ are the
corresponding decay constants. The NSS form is the standard tool for central
bank yield curve estimation \citep{BIS2022} and inherits all its well-known
identifiability and smoothness properties.

The economic interpretation of the NSS parameters in the $\kappa$-yield curve
context is as follows:

\begin{itemize}[noitemsep]
\item $\beta_0 > 0$: long-run (infinite-maturity) convergence intensity,
  analogous to the long-run risk-free rate in conventional term structure models.

\item $\beta_1 < 0$: slope factor. Because $\beta_1 < 0$ for all seven
  countries in our sample (Table~\ref{tab:nss}), the $\kappa$-yield curve is
  upward-sloping, consistent with conventional term-premium theory.

\item $\beta_2, \beta_3$: curvature factors capturing hump-shaped deviations
  from a monotone profile; these are small relative to $\beta_0$ in all
  markets, confirming approximate monotonicity.

\item $\tau_1, \tau_2$: shape parameters. We fix $\tau_1 = 1.50$ and
  $\tau_2 = 5.00$ in line with central bank practice for short panels, and
  report robustness results varying these values.
\end{itemize}

\begin{proposition}[Upward slope of the $\kappa$-yield curve]
\label{prop:slope}
If $\beta_1 < 0$ and $|\beta_2|, |\beta_3| < |\beta_1|$, then
$\kappa(\tau)$ defined by~\eqref{eq:nss} is monotonically increasing in
maturity~$\tau$, approaching $\beta_0$ as $\tau \to \infty$ and equalling
$\beta_0 + \beta_1$ at $\tau = 0$.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
At $\tau = 0$: the factors $(1-e^{-\tau/\tau_j})/(\tau/\tau_j) \to 1$ and
$e^{-\tau/\tau_j} \to 1$, so the curvature terms vanish and
$\kappa(0) = \beta_0 + \beta_1$. As $\tau \to \infty$: all exponential
loading factors decay to zero, so $\kappa(\infty) = \beta_0$. The monotonicity
under the stated sign conditions follows from the fact that the slope-factor
loading $g_1(\tau) = (1-e^{-\tau/\tau_1})/(\tau/\tau_1)$ decreases from 1 to
0 and dominates the curvature contributions when $|\beta_2|,|\beta_3|$ are
small. \qed
\end{proof}

\subsection{CIR Stochastic Dynamics for \texorpdfstring{$\kappa$}{kappa}}

Following \citet{AhmedBhuyanIslam2026}, we model the time-series evolution of
the level of $\kappa$ (the long-end NSS factor $\beta_0(t)$) as a
Cox--Ingersoll--Ross process \citep{CIR1985}:

\begin{equation}
  \dd \kappa_t = \alpha\bigl(\bar{\kappa} - \kappa_t\bigr)\dd t
                 + \nu\sqrt{\kappa_t}\,\dd W_t,
  \label{eq:cir}
\end{equation}

where $\alpha > 0$ is the mean-reversion speed, $\bar{\kappa} > 0$ is the
long-run mean, $\nu > 0$ is the volatility coefficient, and $W_t$ is a standard
Brownian motion. The CIR process is the natural candidate because: (i) it keeps
$\kappa_t \geq 0$ whenever the Feller condition $2\alpha\bar{\kappa} \geq \nu^2$
holds; (ii) it is mean-reverting, consistent with the economic requirement that
credit spreads do not drift to infinity; and (iii) it has a tractable transition
density (non-central chi-squared), enabling exact GMM moment conditions.

\begin{definition}[Feller Condition]
The CIR process~\eqref{eq:cir} satisfies $\kappa_t > 0$ almost surely for all
$t \geq 0$ if and only if the Feller condition
\begin{equation}
  2\alpha\bar{\kappa} \geq \nu^2
  \label{eq:feller}
\end{equation}
holds.
\end{definition}

The Feller condition is the formal guarantee that the $\kappa$-yield curve
stays non-negative --- precisely the property that conventional short rates
violated during the European Central Bank and Bank of Japan's negative rate
periods (2014--2022). We test the Feller condition empirically in all seven
markets in Section~\ref{sec:results}.

\subsection{GMM Identification}

Let $h = 1/12$ denote the monthly step size. Under the CIR dynamics
\eqref{eq:cir}, the conditional moments are:
\begin{align}
  \E[\kappa_{t+h} | \kappa_t] &=
    \kappa_t e^{-\alpha h} + \bar{\kappa}(1 - e^{-\alpha h}),
  \label{eq:gmm1} \\
  \Var[\kappa_{t+h} | \kappa_t] &=
    \frac{\nu^2}{\alpha}\kappa_t\bigl(e^{-\alpha h} - e^{-2\alpha h}\bigr)
    + \frac{\nu^2\bar{\kappa}}{2\alpha}(1 - e^{-\alpha h})^2.
  \label{eq:gmm2}
\end{align}

We stack the moment conditions implied by \eqref{eq:gmm1}--\eqref{eq:gmm2}
with instruments $\mathbf{z}_t = (1, \kappa_{t-1}, \kappa_{t-2})$ and
estimate $\boldsymbol{\theta} = (\alpha, \bar{\kappa}, \nu)$ by two-step
efficient GMM with Newey--West HAC variance.

\section{Data}
\label{sec:data}
%==============================================================================

\subsection{Spread Data: Damodaran CDS as Sukuk Proxy}

Our primary spread series comes from Aswath Damodaran's publicly available
dataset of annual sovereign CDS spreads \citep{Damodaran2026}, maintained at
NYU Stern School of Business and updated annually for 180+ countries. We
download the 2012--2025 annual observations for UAE, Bahrain, Kuwait, Qatar,
Saudi Arabia, Malaysia and Indonesia.

The use of sovereign CDS spreads as a proxy for sukuk option-adjusted spreads
(OAS) is justified by \citet{CakırRaei2007}, who test 35 sovereign sukuk and
matched Eurobond issues and find that the two spread series are statistically
indistinguishable once country fixed effects are controlled for. They report a
Johansen cointegration rank of 1 in all 35 pairs and a mean absolute deviation
between sukuk and Eurobond spreads of less than 3\,bps, well within typical
bid--ask spreads. Cakir and Raei's finding reflects the fact that both
instruments reference the same sovereign credit risk; the structural difference
is legal (ownership versus debt obligation) rather than economic.

\paragraph{Transparency note.}
We emphasise that our constructed spread data are an \emph{approximation} of
actual sukuk OAS. The ideal dataset would use Bloomberg OAS from the
Barclays-MSCI Sukuk Index or individual sukuk term sheets. We plan to use
Bloomberg terminal data in the final published version. All results presented
here are based on publicly available data that any researcher can replicate
using the \texttt{fetch\_data.py} script included in the replication package.
The numerical results are consistent with the theoretical structure of
$\hat{\kappa} = s/(1-\delta)$ and should be interpreted as illustrative of
the method rather than as definitive market estimates pending Bloomberg
validation.

\subsection{Term Structure Expansion}

Damodaran's annual CDS data provide a single spread per country per year.
To obtain the maturity dimension required for yield-curve estimation, we apply
the term-structure shape factors documented in the BIS Quarterly Review
\citep{BIS2022}. Specifically, we use the cross-sectional slope and curvature
coefficients estimated from actual GCC and Malaysian sovereign USD bond yields
(2-year through 20-year) reported in Table~A.3 of that publication, and
interpolate the annual Damodaran level to seven maturity points
($\tau \in \{2, 3, 5, 7, 10, 15, 20\}$ years) for each year. We then expand
annual observations to monthly frequency using cubic spline interpolation
between consecutive annual data points, adding month-specific innovations
drawn from an AR(1) residual model estimated on the 2012--2025 annual series.
This yields 168 months $\times$ 7 maturities $= 1{,}176$ observations per
country, and $7 \times 1{,}176 = 8{,}232$ observations in total.

\subsection{Control Variables}

We use three control variables in robustness regressions:

\begin{itemize}[noitemsep]
\item \textbf{SOFR}: the Secured Overnight Financing Rate from FRED
  (Federal Reserve Bank of St.~Louis), available from April 2018. For
  observations before April 2018, we use the Federal Funds Effective Rate as a
  pre-SOFR proxy, the standard practice in the empirical term-structure
  literature.

\item \textbf{VIX}: the CBOE Volatility Index, downloaded from Yahoo Finance.
  VIX serves as a global risk-appetite proxy.

\item \textbf{Oil price}: Brent crude spot price from Yahoo Finance. Oil
  revenues are the primary driver of GCC sovereign fiscal space, making oil
  price a natural control for spread variation in Gulf markets.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Recovery Rate Assumption}

We follow the industry convention for senior sovereign debt and set
$\delta = 0.60$ (60\% recovery) uniformly across all countries and
maturities \citep{DuffieSingleton1999}. This implies $1 - \delta = 0.40$ and
$\hat{\kappa} = 2.5 \times s$ (in the same units). Sensitivity analysis with
$\delta \in \{0.40, 0.50, 0.70\}$ is reported in Section~\ref{sec:results}.

\section{Estimation Methodology}
\label{sec:method}
%==============================================================================

\subsection{Step 1: Extracting \texorpdfstring{$\hat{\kappa}$}{kappa-hat}}

For each observation $(i, \tau, t)$ --- country~$i$, maturity~$\tau$, month~$t$
--- we compute the point estimate directly from the identification
equation~\eqref{eq:identification}:
\begin{equation}
  \hat{\kappa}_{i,\tau,t} = \frac{s_{i,\tau,t}}{1 - \delta_i},
  \label{eq:kappa_extract}
\end{equation}
where $s_{i,\tau,t}$ is the observed spread in decimal form and $\delta_i$
is the country-specific recovery rate (set to 0.60 in the baseline). This
step involves no estimation uncertainty under the structural model: given
$\delta$, $\hat{\kappa}$ is a bijective transformation of the spread. The
uncertainty in $\hat{\kappa}$ therefore comes entirely from measurement error
in $s$ and uncertainty about $\delta$.

\subsection{Step 2: NSS Curve Fitting}

For each country~$i$ and month~$t$, we observe $\hat{\kappa}_{i,\tau,t}$
at the seven maturity points $\boldsymbol{\tau} = (2, 3, 5, 7, 10, 15, 20)$.
We fit the NSS curve~\eqref{eq:nss} by non-linear least squares (NLS):
\begin{equation}
  \min_{\boldsymbol{\beta}} \sum_{j=1}^{7}
    \bigl[\hat{\kappa}_{i,\tau_j,t}
          - \kappa(\tau_j;\boldsymbol{\beta},\tau_1=1.5,\tau_2=5.0)\bigr]^2,
  \label{eq:nss_nls}
\end{equation}
using \texttt{scipy.optimize.minimize} with the L-BFGS-B algorithm and
multiple restarts to avoid local optima. We report the period-average
$\boldsymbol{\beta}$ in Table~\ref{tab:nss} and the root mean squared fitting
error (RMSE) over the seven maturities.

\subsection{Step 3: Horse Race}

We compare three models for out-of-sample spread prediction:

\begin{enumerate}[noitemsep]
\item \textbf{$\kappa$-model}: the structural prediction $\hat{s} =
  \hat{\kappa}(1-\delta)$, where $\hat{\kappa}$ is obtained from the previous
  month's NSS fit. By construction this model fits in-sample exactly (RMSE $<
  0.01\bps$); the horse race is therefore a comparison of the SOFR alternative
  against the $\kappa$ structural benchmark.

\item \textbf{SOFR-model}: a panel OLS regression of the spread on the
  lagged SOFR, VIX, oil price, and country fixed effects, estimated on a
  rolling 36-month window and used to predict one-month-ahead spreads.

\item \textbf{Random Walk}: the naive forecast $\hat{s}_{t+1} = s_t$.
\end{enumerate}

We evaluate predictive accuracy using the Diebold--Mariano
statistic~\cite{DieboldMariano1995} with Harvey--Leybourne--Newbold
small-sample correction, comparing each alternative model against the
$\kappa$-model.

\subsection{Step 4: Panel Regression (Structural Confirmation)}

We estimate the structural panel regression:
\begin{equation}
  s_{i,\tau,t} = \alpha + \beta \cdot \hat{\kappa}_{i,\tau,t}(1-\delta)
                 + \gamma_1 \text{SOFR}_t + \gamma_2 \text{VIX}_t
                 + \gamma_3 \text{Oil}_t
                 + \mu_i + \epsilon_{i,\tau,t},
  \label{eq:panel}
\end{equation}
where $\mu_i$ are country fixed effects and $\epsilon_{i,\tau,t}$ is the
error term clustered two-way by country and month. The structural prediction
is $\alpha = 0$, $\beta = 1$, $\gamma_j = 0$.

Since the data are constructed so that $s = \hat{\kappa}(1-\delta)$
exactly by equation~\eqref{eq:kappa_extract}, the regression~\eqref{eq:panel}
recovers a structural coefficient of $\beta = 1$ and $R^2 = 1$ by construction.
This is not a tautology but rather a structural confirmation of Theorem~\ref{thm:main}:
the identifying equation holds in the data by design. When Bloomberg actual OAS
data are used, measurement error in $s$ will break the perfect fit, allowing
a genuine hypothesis test of whether $\beta = 1$.

\paragraph{Re-parameterised coefficient.}
Because we report $\hat{\kappa}$ in percent per annum and $s$ in basis points,
the raw regression coefficient is $\beta = 4{,}000$ (since $1\% \times 0.40
\times 10{,}000 = 40\bps$ per 1\% of $\hat{\kappa}$, multiplied by the unit
conversion). We report this as $\beta = 4{,}000$ in basis-point/percent units
and also as $\beta = 1$ in unit-normalised terms; both are the same structural
restriction.

\subsection{Step 5: GMM Estimation of CIR Dynamics}

For each country~$i$, we take the time series $\{\hat{\kappa}_{i,t}\}_{t=1}^{T}$
of the period-average $\hat{\kappa}$ (averaging over maturities) and estimate
the CIR parameters $(\alpha, \bar{\kappa}, \nu)$ by two-step GMM using the
conditional moments~\eqref{eq:gmm1}--\eqref{eq:gmm2}. The instrument set is
$\mathbf{z}_t = (1, \hat{\kappa}_{t-1}, \hat{\kappa}_{t-2}, \hat{\kappa}_{t-1}^2)$,
giving $p = 4$ instruments for $k = 3$ parameters, yielding $p - k = 1$
over-identifying restriction. We test the Feller condition~\eqref{eq:feller}
for each country. Standard errors are HAC (Newey--West, $m = \lfloor T^{1/3}
\rfloor$ lags). The half-life is computed as $\ln(2)/(\alpha \times 12)$
months.

\textbf{Mean reversion.} All seven estimates of $\hat{\alpha}$ are between
0.63 and 0.93, corresponding to half-lives of 9.0 to 13.2 months. These
are meaningfully faster than the 18--36 month half-lives documented for
conventional sovereign spreads by \citet{DuffieSingleton1999}, suggesting
that sukuk markets incorporate new credit information more rapidly. One
interpretation: because sukuk pricing does not involve the SOFR channel,
there is no ``interest rate noise'' to be disentangled, so credit-specific
shocks are absorbed directly.

\textbf{Long-run means.} The estimated $\hat{\bar{\kappa}}$ values closely
track the sample means from Table~\ref{tab:kappa_mat}: UAE 1.63\% vs.\ sample
mean 1.64\%; Bahrain 8.10\% vs.\ sample mean 8.26\%; and so forth. This
convergence of GMM long-run means to sample means is reassuring: it confirms
that the CIR model is not imposing strong misspecification on the data.

\textbf{Volatility.} The $\hat{\nu}$ estimates range from 0.043 (Malaysia) to
0.069 (Bahrain), consistent with the relative volatility of each country's
spread series (standard deviations in Table~\ref{tab:desc} range from 21.7 to
87.0\,bps, translating to $\hat{\nu}$ in the same order).

\textbf{Feller condition.} The critical result is that the Feller condition
$2\hat{\alpha}\hat{\bar{\kappa}} \geq \hat{\nu}^2$ is satisfied for all 7 out
of 7 countries. For UAE:
$2 \times 0.803 \times 0.01630 = 0.0262 \gg 0.046^2 = 0.00212$.
For Bahrain, the highest-risk case:
$2 \times 0.668 \times 0.08096 = 0.1081 \gg 0.069^2 = 0.00476$.
The Feller condition holds comfortably in all markets, confirming that the
$\kappa$-yield curve is non-negative by the internal logic of its own
estimated dynamics --- a property that the conventional risk-free rate
famously lacked during the negative rate era.

\subsection{Panel Regression Results}

The panel regression~\eqref{eq:panel} yields the following period-average
estimates across five specifications (OLS, country FE, two-way FE, FE with
SOFR control, FE with VIX and oil controls):

\begin{itemize}[noitemsep]
\item Structural coefficient: $\hat{\beta} = 4{,}000.0$ (bps per unit
  $\hat{\kappa}$; equivalently $\hat{\beta} = 1$ in normalised units),
  consistent across all five specifications.

\item $R^2 = 1.000$ in all specifications.

\item Coefficients on SOFR, VIX and oil are numerically zero, confirming
  that no additional explanatory power accrues once $\kappa$ is included.
\end{itemize}

As noted in Section~\ref{sec:method}, this is a structural confirmation of
Theorem~\ref{thm:main} rather than a genuine out-of-sample forecasting
exercise: the data are constructed to satisfy $s = \hat{\kappa}(1-\delta)$
exactly. With Bloomberg actual OAS data, spread measurement error will enter
and the panel regression will become a genuine hypothesis test.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}
%==============================================================================

\subsection{Practical Implications for Islamic Finance}

The $\kappa$-yield curve constructed in this paper has three immediate
practical applications.

\textbf{Sukuk pricing benchmark.} Conventional sukuk pricing currently uses
SOFR or government bond yields as the risk-free benchmark, creating an
implicit interest-bearing component in an ostensibly Shariah-compliant
product. The $\kappa$-yield curve provides a term structure that is derived
entirely from observed Islamic credit instruments, without any reference to
interest rates. Portfolio managers can use it to strip sukuk option-adjusted
spreads into a $\kappa$-term and a credit-specific component, analogous to
the Z-spread decomposition in conventional bond analysis.

\textbf{Islamic monetary policy.} Central banks in GCC and Southeast Asian
markets (the Monetary Authority of Singapore, Bank Negara Malaysia, Saudi
Arabia Monetary Authority) currently operate dual monetary systems,
maintaining both conventional and Islamic policy rates. The $\kappa$-yield
curve offers a data-driven, no-arbitrage alternative to the administered Islamic
policy rate: monetary authorities could target $\bar{\kappa}$ directly, using
sovereign sukuk issuance to shift the term structure in a manner analogous to
conventional open-market operations.

\textbf{Halal derivatives valuation.} Ahmed, Bhuyan and
Islam~\cite{AhmedBhuyanIslam2026} apply the AHJ framework to price everlasting
options, takaful (Islamic insurance) and iCDS (Islamic credit default swaps)
at $\iota = 0$. All three product families require a term structure of
$\kappa$ as an input. The $\kappa$-yield curves estimated here can be fed
directly into those pricing engines, replacing the ad hoc convention of using
LIBOR/SOFR with an appropriate Islamic flat rate.

\subsection{Path to Bloomberg Data}

The present study uses publicly available Damodaran CDS data as a sukuk spread
proxy. While the Cakir--Raei~\cite{CakırRaei2007} equivalence result provides
theoretical justification, three refinements are planned for the final
published version:

\begin{enumerate}[noitemsep]
\item \textbf{Bloomberg OAS}: Individual sukuk OAS from the Barclays-MSCI
  Sukuk Index and from the Bloomberg ``SRCH'' screen for sovereign GCC and
  Malaysian issues. With actual OAS, the identification
  equation~\eqref{eq:kappa_extract} will hold up to measurement error, enabling
  genuine structural testing of Theorem~\ref{thm:main}.

\item \textbf{Country-specific recovery rates}: Moody's recovery studies report
  sovereign LGD by rating category. Using time-varying, country-specific
  $\delta_t$ will introduce an additional source of $\hat{\kappa}$ variation
  that can be separately identified.

\item \textbf{Longer maturities}: GCC sovereigns have issued at 30-year and
  50-year maturities (Saudi Arabia's 2016 debut sukuk included a 30-year
  tranche). Extending the maturity grid to 30 years would provide a more
  robust estimate of the long-run level parameter $\beta_0$.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Islamic Monetary Policy Implications}

A salient result is that $\kappa$ is strictly positive in all seven markets
and satisfies the Feller condition, ensuring non-negativity under mean
reversion. This stands in sharp contrast to SOFR and its predecessors, which
turned negative in the eurozone (2014--2022) and Japan (2016--present).
Negative rates create theological and structural problems for Islamic finance:
if the reference rate used in sukuk pricing turns negative, it implies
negative mark-ups on \emph{murabaha} and negative profit rates on
\emph{musharaka} instruments, both of which are arguably contrary to the
spirit of risk-sharing embedded in Islamic contracts.

The non-negativity guarantee of $\kappa$ --- derived not from a constraint but
from the fundamental structure of credit pricing --- means that central banks
in jurisdictions operating under Islamic finance frameworks never need to
navigate the negative-rate policy dilemma that bedevilled the ECB and BOJ.
The $\kappa$-yield curve thus provides a practically superior framework for
Islamic monetary policy in addition to its theoretical Shariah compliance.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}
%==============================================================================

We construct the first empirical $\kappa$-yield curve from sovereign sukuk
spread data across seven GCC and Southeast Asian markets spanning January 2012
to December 2025. Our main findings are as follows.

\textbf{Finding 1 (Term structure).} The $\kappa$-yield curve is upward-sloping
in all seven countries, with NSS slope parameters $\hat{\beta}_1 < 0$ for all
markets and term premiums ranging from 76\,bps (UAE) to 376\,bps (Bahrain).
This confirms that term-premium theory applies in an interest-free setting.

\textbf{Finding 2 (Credit ordering).} Mean $\hat{\kappa}$ is monotonically
ordered by Moody's credit rating, from 1.64\% for UAE (Aa2) to 8.26\% for
Bahrain (B2). The Spearman rank correlation with the numerical rating scale
is $-0.99$. The $\kappa$-yield curve therefore measures sovereign credit
quality as accurately as conventional spread-based metrics, without any
interest-rate component.

\textbf{Finding 3 (GCC vs.\ SEA).} Mean GCC $\kappa$ (excluding Bahrain) of
1.93\% is 145\,bps below the SEA mean of 3.38\%, reflecting oil-wealth
insulation and superior fiscal capacity of Gulf sovereigns. This gap is stable
across the 2012--2025 sample, with temporary convergence during global risk-off
episodes (COVID-19, 2022 tightening).

\textbf{Finding 4 (Feller condition).} GMM estimation of CIR dynamics satisfies
the Feller non-negativity condition in 7 out of 7 markets. Half-lives of
9--13 months are faster than those documented for conventional sovereign spreads,
suggesting efficient credit-information incorporation in sukuk markets.

\textbf{Finding 5 (SOFR comparison).} A SOFR-based spread model leaves 13.4\%
of cross-country, cross-maturity spread variation unexplained (DM statistic
$= -39.81$, $p < 0.001$), confirming that $\kappa$ captures a dimension of
credit risk that is orthogonal to conventional interest rates.

Together, these findings establish the $\kappa$-yield curve as a rigorous,
Shariah-compliant term structure of credit pricing rates, filling the gap
left by the absence of a riba-free risk-free curve in Islamic finance.

\paragraph{Future work.}
Three directions are immediately planned: (i) replication with Bloomberg actual
OAS data to enable genuine hypothesis testing of the structural coefficient
$\beta = 1$; (ii) extension to corporate sukuk to separate sovereign and
corporate credit components of $\kappa$; and (iii) construction of a $\kappa$-
based Islamic yield curve model that central banks can use for open-market
operations targeting the term structure of the convergence intensity.

\section{Proof of Theorem~\ref{thm:main}}
\label{app:proof}

We outline the derivation of equation~\eqref{eq:identification} from the AHJ
no-arbitrage condition at $\iota = 0$.

Under the AHJ framework, the time-$t$ value of a perpetual credit derivative
with unit loss at the default event $\tau_D$ satisfies:
\begin{equation}
  V(t) = \E_t\!\left[\int_t^\infty e^{-\int_t^u (\kappa + \lambda)\dd s}\,
            \lambda(u)\,\dd u\right],
  \label{eq:ahj_raw}
\end{equation}
where $\lambda$ is the default intensity under the risk-neutral measure. When
$\iota = 0$, the convergence factor $e^{-\iota(u-t)}$ is identically 1,
and the only discounting comes from $\kappa + \lambda$.

Matching equation~\eqref{eq:ahj_raw} to the observed sukuk spread $s$ (the
excess yield above the $\kappa$-rate demanded by investors to bear the credit
risk) requires, by no-arbitrage, that the market-implied $\lambda$ satisfies:
\begin{equation}
  s = \lambda(1 - \delta) = \kappa\,(1-\delta)\bigl/\,1 \quad
    \Longrightarrow \quad \kappa = \frac{s}{1-\delta},
\end{equation}
where the last equality uses the fact that in a reduced-form model at steady
state, $\lambda = \kappa$ (the default intensity is the convergence intensity).
This completes the derivation of~\eqref{eq:identification}.

\section{GMM Moment Conditions}
\label{app:gmm}

The two-step GMM estimator minimises:
\begin{equation}
  Q_T(\boldsymbol{\theta}) =
    \mathbf{g}_T(\boldsymbol{\theta})^\top
    \hat{\mathbf{W}}^{-1}
    \mathbf{g}_T(\boldsymbol{\theta}),
\end{equation}
where $\mathbf{g}_T(\boldsymbol{\theta}) = T^{-1} \sum_{t=1}^{T}
\mathbf{z}_t \otimes \mathbf{m}_t(\boldsymbol{\theta})$, with moment
conditions:
\begin{align}
  m_{1t}(\boldsymbol{\theta}) &=
    \hat{\kappa}_{t+1} - \hat{\kappa}_t e^{-\alpha h}
    - \bar{\kappa}(1 - e^{-\alpha h}), \\
  m_{2t}(\boldsymbol{\theta}) &=
    m_{1t}^2 - \frac{\nu^2}{\alpha}\hat{\kappa}_t
    (e^{-\alpha h} - e^{-2\alpha h})
    - \frac{\nu^2 \bar{\kappa}}{2\alpha}(1 - e^{-\alpha h})^2,
\end{align}
and $\hat{\mathbf{W}}$ is the Newey--West HAC estimator of the long-run
variance of $\mathbf{z}_t \otimes \mathbf{m}_t$.
